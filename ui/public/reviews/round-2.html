Reviewer A
Tech: 6.0 Nov: 4.0 Clar: 9.0 Sig: 7.0 Overall: 6.5
Technical Quality (6.0/10)
The paper poses a clean comparative question and provides empirical evidence, explicit assumptions, and a useful ablation over judge bias. The cost accounting and saturation argument are relevant, and the main claims are at least partially supported by the reported tables and bootstrap intervals. However, from a rigor standpoint the technical formulation is not fully specified: the learned descriptor training lacks essential optimization details, the DIRECT fallback rule for uncached pairs is methodologically questionable, and the paper makes strong mechanistic claims ('bias compounds') without formal analysis. Several choices that materially affect the outcome are under-justified, including the 2D descriptor restriction, fixed greedy selection, and the asymmetric handling of incomplete-budget behavior. I therefore find the work technically interesting but not yet at the level of airtight correctness or methodological soundness.
Novelty (4.0/10)
The paper's main novelty is in the experimental framing: a controlled comparison between distilling judge feedback into a descriptor and using judge feedback directly at selection time within a low-volume FoO regime. That is a worthwhile angle, but the methodological ingredients are largely inherited from prior work, and the contribution is primarily an empirical crossover observation under a synthetic biased-judge model. The novelty claims are somewhat overstated because the judge model is stylized, related alternatives are not discussed comprehensively, and fairness questions in the comparison weaken the strength of the claimed new insight. As presented, this feels more like a targeted empirical note or ablation-driven follow-up than a substantial methodological advance.
Clarity (9.0/10)
The paper is well written, easy to follow, and structured around a clear problem statement, method, and takeaway. The introduction motivates the comparison effectively, and the results section communicates the crossover narrative cleanly. Tables and the referenced figure are integrated sensibly and the main empirical message is straightforward to understand. My main clarity reservation is that some notation and naming are overloaded or inconsistent, especially the reuse of B for budget and within the pairwise-count expression, plus minor ambiguity around exact training details and stopping conditions. Overall, though, the exposition is strong.
Significance (7.0/10)
The question is important: how to use expensive and potentially biased AI feedback in diversity-oriented selection is highly relevant, and the paper identifies a regime where standard amortization intuitions may fail. If the central claim survives validation with real LLM judges, it could influence design choices for small candidate-set QD systems and FoO-like reasoning pipelines. That said, the present evidence is restricted to a synthetic benchmark with a narrow setting, so the practical significance is still provisional. The work opens useful follow-up directions, but the current results are not yet strong enough to justify broad adoption or a field-wide shift in approach.
Overall Verdict (6.5/10)
This paper asks a sharp and relevant question and presents a plausible empirical finding: in small-option FoO settings with biased judge feedback, direct querying can outperform descriptor distillation at sufficiently high budget. The paper is clear and the experimental story is coherent, but the rigor is not yet strong enough for me to fully trust the conclusion. In particular, the synthetic judge model, incomplete specification of training and selection details, and potentially consequential fairness issues in how the two paradigms are operationalized limit confidence. My recommendation is weak reject in its current form, with encouragement to strengthen the methodological specification and validate the result with a real LLM judge.

Technical Quality (4.0/10)

Fairness of the baselines is also questionable because the so-called single-path policy includes a penalty for agreement with rivals, making it not purely confirmatory, while the candidate hypothesis class exactly matches the simulator laws, which substantially simplifies the problem. Overall, the experiments are suggestive but not technically decisive.

The proposed gate is very close in spirit to standard disagreement-based active selection, and the paper itself acknowledges this. Positioning against the broader active learning and experimental design literature is only partial, and the related-work discussion is too thin to establish that this specific formulation is meaningfully distinct from existing disagreement, query-by-committee, or information-based criteria. The claims are not wildly overstated, but the contribution reads more as a narrow benchmark study plus ablation than a substantial new method.

Significance (6.0/10)

The practical effect sizes reported within the simulator are large, and the emphasis on discriminating experiments rather than confirmatory ones is conceptually relevant beyond this toy setting. That said, significance is limited by the narrowness of the benchmark and the exact-match synthetic hypothesis class. Because all evidence comes from one stylized simulator family with five hand-designed laws and a small budget, it is hard to judge whether the conclusion will materially influence broader work on agentic science or active experimental design. I find the empirical signal plausible, yet the technical standards are not high enough for strong confidence.
