Reviewer A
Tech: 0.0 Nov: 5.0 Clar: 8.0 Sig: 8.0 Overall: 5.2
Technical Quality (0.0/10)
From a rigor standpoint, this paper is far below the standard required for strong technical acceptance. The core proposal is evaluated only in a synthetic linear sandbox specifically constructed so that a linear contrastive projection can succeed; the paper explicitly concedes this optimism, which substantially weakens the evidentiary value of the reported gains. There are no formal guarantees, no proofs, no statistical significance testing, no confidence intervals, no effect sizes, no uncertainty quantification, and no power analysis. Key assumptions such as the linear entanglement model, the favoritism parameter, the oracle construction, the thresholding for duplicates, and the novelty-selection design are not theoretically justified or stress-tested in a comprehensive sensitivity analysis. Experimental description is insufficient for exact replication, and baseline coverage is narrow: the comparison omits stronger diversity-aware selection methods and does not evaluate on actual FoO-generated options or realistic human/LLM similarity supervision. Because the main claims depend on an idealized setup and several unverified leaps from sandbox behavior to real LLM-option behavior, I cannot judge the technical claims as adequately validated.
Novelty (5.0/10)
The idea of importing a learned diversity metric into the FoO option layer is a sensible cross-pollination of two existing lines of work, and that synthesis is the paper's main novel aspect. The authors do give credit for the key ingredients and are fairly explicit that they borrow QDHF's metric-learning insight rather than claiming to invent it. However, the contribution is still fairly incremental: learned similarity metrics, novelty-based selection, and duplicate filtering are all established ideas, and the paper mainly adapts them to a new setting without demonstrating a broader algorithmic leap. Related-work positioning is decent but not comprehensive, and the empirical comparisons are too narrow to fully substantiate the strength of the novelty claim relative to other diversity-aware selection mechanisms.
Clarity (8.0/10)
The paper is generally well written and easy to follow. The motivation is sharp, the central thesis is stated clearly, and the organization from problem diagnosis to method to benchmark to limitations is coherent. Notation is mostly consistent and the methodological sketch is understandable to an expert reader. Results are interpreted candidly, with unusually explicit acknowledgement of boundary conditions. The main weakness in clarity is that several claimed figures/tables are described textually rather than supported by actual visual detail in the submission body, and some implementation choices that matter for interpretation are left underspecified. Even so, as a piece of scientific exposition, it is substantially clearer than average.
Significance (8.0/10)
The problem addressed is important: if FoO-style systems rely on diversity among options, then measuring diversity rather than merely counting options is a meaningful concern. The proposed perspective could plausibly influence how researchers think about option generation, duplicate suppression, and exploration in LLM-agent pipelines. The reported improvements within the benchmark are large enough to suggest practical value if they transfer. That said, the significance is currently limited by the fact that the evidence is confined to a stylized synthetic environment rather than a real FoO deployment; thus the work is more significant as a research direction than as a demonstrated solution. Still, the framing is timely and opens several worthwhile follow-up questions.
Overall Verdict (5.2/10)
This submission presents a clear and interesting idea: Flow-of-Options should not merely enforce diversity structurally, but should learn a metric for what counts as genuinely different. I find the framing compelling and the writing strong, but the technical validation is not sufficient for acceptance under a rigorous standard. The evidence comes entirely from an idealized synthetic benchmark whose structure strongly favors the proposed linear method, with limited baselines and no statistical rigor beyond reporting averages. My recommendation is rejection in its current form, while encouraging a future version that validates the approach on real FoO option sets, realistic similarity supervision, stronger baselines, and substantially more rigorous experimental methodology.

I would recommend it as a valuable follow-on contribution, with the main request being stronger real-task validation in future iterations.
